## Why This Intelligence Matters for Decision Making

**Decision-making value**: Evaluation factors drive THE MOST CRITICAL proposal decisions:

- **Resource Allocation**: 40% weight factor gets 40% of proposal effort (page allocation, staffing, review time)
- **Page Distribution**: 25 pages for 4 subfactors = 6 pages per subfactor (strategic content prioritization)
- **Win Theme Development**: High-weight factors reveal what government values (align win themes accordingly)
- **Competitive Strategy**: Adjectival scoring (DoD) requires different approach than numerical scoring (civilian)
- **Proposal Organization**: Mirror RFP evaluation structure for ease of scoring (evaluator-friendly = better scores)

**Without this intelligence**: Generic factor extraction ("Factor 1: Technical Approach exists")  
**With this intelligence**: Strategic factor extraction ("Factor 1: Technical = 40% weight, 4 subfactors, adjectival scoring, allocate 6 pages per subfactor, emphasize risk mitigation (35% subfactor weight)")

## Common Evaluation Structures (Pattern Recognition)

> **CRITICAL**: These patterns represent COMMON observations from federal RFPs, NOT universal requirements. RFPs vary widely. Use semantic understanding to recognize evaluation INTENT.

### Pattern 1: Three-Factor Trinity (Observed in ~80% of competitive best-value RFPs)

**Decision-making value**: Recognizing this pattern enables immediate resource allocation decisions (technical team size, management team size, past performance volume effort).

**Factor indicators** (recognize semantically):

**Factor 1: Technical Capability** (Names vary):

- "Technical Approach" OR "Solution Design" OR "Methodology" OR "Project Approach" OR "Technical Solution"
- **Semantic meaning**: HOW work will be done (technical execution)
- **Typical weight**: 30-50% (highest single factor in most RFPs)
- **Common subfactors**: Architecture, innovation, risk mitigation, compliance, security
- **Resource allocation**: If 40% weight → allocate 40% of proposal budget/staff here

**Factor 2: Management Capability** (Names vary):

- "Management Approach" OR "Program Management" OR "Team Organization" OR "Management Plan" OR "Project Controls"
- **Semantic meaning**: HOW work will be managed (organizational capability)
- **Typical weight**: 20-35%
- **Common subfactors**: Staffing, quality assurance, schedule, subcontractor oversight, transition
- **Resource allocation**: If 30% weight → allocate 30% of proposal budget/staff here

**Factor 3: Past Performance** (Names vary):

- "Past Performance" OR "Relevant Experience" OR "Corporate Experience" OR "Contract History" OR "References"
- **Semantic meaning**: PROOF of capability (track record)
- **Typical weight**: 20-40%
- **Common criteria**: Relevance, recency, quality (CPARS ratings)
- **Resource allocation**: If 30% weight → allocate 30% of proposal budget/staff here

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|Factor 1: Technical Approach|evaluation_factor|Technical Approach (Three-Factor Trinity pattern: Technical 40%, Management 30%, Past Performance 30%). Primary evaluation factor in DoD best-value structure. Evaluates HOW contractor will execute technical work (solution architecture, methodology, risk mitigation). Typical subfactors: Solution Architecture, Innovation, Risk Mitigation, Compliance, Security. Adjectival scoring (Outstanding/Good/Acceptable) per FAR 15.305. Page allocation: 40% of total proposal effort = [X] pages from [Y]-page Technical volume. Recognize semantic variants: "Solution Design", "Methodology", "Project Approach", "Technical Solution" = same meaning.
```

**Proposal Resource Allocation Decision**:

```
Three-Factor Trinity (40/30/30) for 90-day proposal:
- Technical (40%): 12 staff x 36 days = 432 staff-days
- Management (30%): 9 staff x 27 days = 243 staff-days
- Past Performance (30%): 9 staff x 27 days = 243 staff-days
- Pricing (separate): 6 staff x 15 days = 90 staff-days
Total: 1,008 staff-days proposal effort
```

### Pattern 1A: Additional Specialized Evaluation Factors

> **CRITICAL**: Beyond the Three-Factor Trinity, many RFPs include specialized factors based on contract type, mission criticality, or agency priorities. Recognize these semantically.

**Decision-making value**: Specialized factors often reveal customer pain points (e.g., Transition factor = incumbent performance issues, Security factor = sensitive data concerns). Recognizing these enables targeted win themes.

#### Transition Approach/Plan (Common in successor contracts)

**Detection signals**:

- "Transition Plan" OR "Phase-In Plan" OR "Takeover Approach" OR "Knowledge Transfer Plan"
- Often appears when: Incumbent mentioned, "current contractor", "continuity of operations" emphasized
- **Semantic meaning**: HOW contractor will assume work from incumbent without service disruption

**Typical weight**: 15-25% of total evaluation (high weight signals transition risk concern)

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|Factor 4: Transition Plan|evaluation_factor|Transition Plan (SPECIALIZED factor - signals successor contract with transition risk concerns). Evaluates: Knowledge transfer approach, continuity of operations, phase-in methodology, incumbent coordination, risk mitigation for service gaps. Weight: 20% (HIGH - indicates government concern about transition risk, likely due to incumbent performance issues or complex system). Subfactors: Knowledge Capture (30%), Continuity Planning (25%), Phase-In Schedule (20%), Risk Mitigation (25%). Decision-making implications: 20% weight = major proposal investment (equivalent to past performance in Trinity model), dedicated transition team required (8+ FTEs for 90-day transition), transition plan detail expected (Gantt chart, knowledge capture methodology, contingency plans). Competitive strategy: Transition excellence can be PRIMARY win theme if RFP emphasizes this factor. Past performance: Prioritize contracts with successful transitions (especially difficult/uncooperative incumbent scenarios). Page allocation: 20% weight with dedicated Transition volume = 15-20 pages detailed transition plan.
```

**Proposal implications**:

- Dedicated Transition volume often required (separate from Technical/Management)
- Transition team resumes (specialized role = different quals than steady-state staff)
- Gantt chart with 30-60-90 day milestones (visual clarity for evaluators)
- Risk register specific to transition risks (service gaps, knowledge loss, incumbent non-cooperation)

#### Small Business Participation Plan (Increasingly evaluated, not just compliance)

**Detection signals**:

- "Small Business Participation Plan" OR "Small Business Utilization Plan" OR "Small Business Integration Strategy" as EVALUATION FACTOR (not just Section I requirement)
- Keywords: "meaningful participation", "SB goal achievement", "mentor-protégé", "teaming approach", "SB integration"
- **Semantic meaning**: HOW contractor will meaningfully integrate small businesses into contract execution (strategy beyond compliance)

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION**:

- **Small Business Participation Plan** (EVALUATION FACTOR): Strategic plan for SB integration, teaming, capability development, meaningful participation
- **Small Business Subcontracting Plan** (COMPLIANCE REQUIREMENT): FAR 52.219-9 compliance document in Section I (goals, good faith effort, reporting)

**Typical weight**: 10-20% (growing trend in 2024-2025 per acquisition.gov guidance)

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|Factor 5: Small Business Participation Plan|evaluation_factor|Small Business Participation Plan (SPECIALIZED factor - evaluates SB INTEGRATION STRATEGY, not just FAR 52.219-9 subcontracting plan compliance). CRITICAL: This is EVALUATION FACTOR (Section M), distinct from Small Business Subcontracting Plan (Section I compliance document). Recent trend: SBA/DoD emphasis on meaningful SB participation as evaluation factor (2024-2025 guidance per acquisition.gov). Evaluates: Integration strategy (HOW SBs are incorporated into team), teaming approach (which SB teammates, what roles, decision-making authority), past performance with SB partnerships (relationship quality, not just percentages), mentor-protégé relationships (capability development), meaningful work allocation (not just pass-through subcontracting). Weight: 15% (SIGNIFICANT - government prioritizing SB participation beyond minimum compliance). Subfactors: Integration Strategy (30%), Teaming Approach (25%), Past SB Performance (30%), Mentor-Protégé Programs (15%). Decision-making implications: Must identify SB teammates BEFORE proposal (cannot add after submission), SB percentages must be realistic (40-50% total typical: 8(a) 5%, HUBZone 3%, SDVOSB 3%, WOSB 5%, SB 25%), demonstrate MEANINGFUL work (not administrative tasks), past performance with SB partnerships critical (relationship quality, joint ventures, teaming success). Competitive differentiation: Exceed minimum goals (propose 45% when minimum is 40% = shows commitment), established SB relationships (LOIs from teammates, prior teaming history), mentor-protégé programs (capability development story), SB decision-making roles (not just labor). Risk: Over-promise SB percentages = non-compliance post-award = CPARS downgrade, "pass-through" subcontracting (SBs get clerical work only) = low evaluation score. Proposal strategy: Detailed SB integration plan (which subs do WHAT WORK with decision authority, not just percentages), SB teammate letters of intent, past performance narrative on SB partnerships (emphasize relationship quality, joint problem-solving, teaming success stories), demonstrate meaningful work allocation (technical roles, management roles, not just admin support).
```

**Proposal implications**:

- SB teammates identified with signed LOIs/teaming agreements (pre-proposal commitment)
- Detailed work breakdown showing SB MEANINGFUL scopes (technical/management roles, not just admin support)
- Past performance data on SB partnerships (relationship quality, joint ventures, teaming success - not just compliance percentages)
- Mentor-protégé program descriptions if applicable (shows SB capability development)
- Distinction from Section I Subcontracting Plan: Participation Plan = strategic integration, Subcontracting Plan = compliance document

### Pattern 2: Adjectival vs Numerical Scoring

**Decision-making value**: Scoring methodology drives competitive strategy. Adjectival (DoD) = demonstrate clear superiority. Numerical (civilian) = accumulate points across many criteria.

#### Adjectival Scoring (Common in DoD RFPs)

**Detection signals**:

- Keywords: "Outstanding", "Good", "Acceptable", "Marginal", "Unacceptable"
- Keywords: "Excellent", "Very Good", "Satisfactory", "Unsatisfactory"
- Phrase patterns: "adjectival ratings", "color ratings", "performance confidence assessment"

**Scoring mechanics** (FAR 15.305 Source Selection):

- **Outstanding**: Exceeds requirements, numerous strengths, minimal-to-no risk, high probability of success
- **Good**: Meets requirements, mix of strengths/weaknesses, low-to-moderate risk, high probability of success
- **Acceptable**: Meets minimum requirements, weaknesses offset by strengths, moderate risk, acceptable probability of success
- **Marginal**: Does not meet requirements in one or more areas, significant weaknesses, high risk
- **Unacceptable**: Does not meet requirements, unacceptable risk, low probability of success

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|Factor 1: Technical Approach|evaluation_factor|Technical Approach evaluated using ADJECTIVAL scoring per FAR 15.305 (Outstanding/Good/Acceptable/Marginal/Unacceptable). DoD pattern: Outstanding = EXCEEDS requirements with numerous strengths and minimal risk. Good = MEETS requirements with some strengths. Acceptable = MINIMUM compliance (rarely wins in competitive environment). Competitive strategy: Must achieve Outstanding or strong Good to win - Acceptable rarely competitive. Demonstrate clear superiority over minimum: quantified performance targets (e.g., "99.99% uptime" vs RFP minimum "99.9%"), innovation beyond baseline, comprehensive risk mitigation. Evaluators look for: technical depth, specificity (not generic), proof points from past performance.
```

**Competitive Strategy Implications**:

- **Outstanding required to win**: In competitive environment, Acceptable = lose (rarely wins)
- **Strength accumulation**: Need multiple strengths to offset any weaknesses
- **Risk mitigation**: Comprehensive risk identification + mitigation plans = higher confidence rating
- **Differentiation**: Must show superiority over minimum requirements (not just compliance)

#### Numerical Scoring (Common in Civilian RFPs)

**Detection signals**:

- Keywords: "points", "100-point scale", "weighted scoring", "0-5 rating"
- Phrase patterns: "maximum points available", "technical merit score", "point allocation"

**Scoring mechanics**:

- Points allocated per subfactor: "Factor 1: Technical (40 points max), Factor 2: Management (30 points max)"
- Evaluators score 0-[max] based on proposal quality
- Total score = sum of factor scores, weighted if applicable

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|Factor 2: Management Approach|evaluation_factor|Management Approach evaluated on NUMERICAL scale (0-30 points maximum). Civilian agency pattern: Points awarded for specific elements (staffing plan = 10 pts, QA = 8 pts, schedule = 7 pts, sub mgmt = 5 pts). Competitive strategy: Accumulate points across ALL criteria - cannot ignore any subfactor (vs adjectival where Outstanding in one area can offset weakness elsewhere). Address EVERY evaluation element explicitly. Use tables/matrices for scoring ease (labor category matrix, risk register, schedule Gantt, QA checklist). Evaluator-friendly proposal = easier scoring = more points.
```

**Competitive Strategy Implications**:

- **Address every criterion**: Cannot skip subfactors (lose points)
- **Scoring rubrics**: If RFP provides scoring rubric, mirror it exactly in proposal
- **Evaluator ease**: Use tables/matrices to show compliance (easy to score = more points)
- **Point optimization**: Emphasize high-point subfactors, ensure minimum compliance on low-point subfactors

### Pattern 3: Price/Cost Evaluation Methodology

**Decision-making value**: Price methodology determines technical proposal strategy. LPTA = minimal technical effort (pass/fail). Best Value = invest heavily in technical differentiation.

#### Best Value Tradeoff (Most Common)

**Detection signals**:

- "best value to the Government"
- "tradeoff between technical merit and cost"
- "technical superiority may justify higher cost"

**Scoring mechanics**:

- Technical factors scored independently of price
- Source Selection Authority (SSA) performs tradeoff: Is Technical Proposal A's superiority worth its 15% price premium over Proposal B?
- Higher price can win if technical superiority justifies premium

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|Price Evaluation - Best Value Tradeoff|concept|Best Value Tradeoff methodology per FAR 15.101-1. Technical factors evaluated independently of price. SSA performs tradeoff: Is technical superiority worth price premium? Typical pattern: "Award to highest technically rated unless lower-rated proposal offers significant price savings without unacceptable technical risk." Pricing strategy: Price to win (competitive but not lowest), invest heavily in technical differentiation, demonstrate value (ROI, cost avoidance, efficiency gains). Higher price acceptable if Outstanding technical rating vs Good for lower price. Avoid: Lowest price without technical superiority (rarely wins in best value). Show: Cost-benefit analysis in proposal (our 10% higher price delivers 30% better outcomes).
```

**Pricing Strategy Implications**:

- **Don't race to bottom**: Lowest price doesn't automatically win
- **Invest in technical**: Outstanding technical rating can justify 10-20% price premium
- **Show value**: Quantify benefits that offset higher price (cost avoidance, faster delivery, lower risk)
- **Sweet spot**: Competitive pricing + Outstanding technical = winning combination

#### LPTA (Lowest Price Technically Acceptable)

**Detection signals**:

- "lowest price technically acceptable"
- "technical proposal is pass/fail"
- "award to lowest-priced acceptable offeror"

**Scoring mechanics**:

- Technical proposal scored pass/fail (acceptable vs unacceptable)
- All acceptable proposals compete on price alone
- Lowest price wins (technical quality beyond "acceptable" doesn't matter)

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|Price Evaluation - LPTA|concept|Lowest Price Technically Acceptable (LPTA) methodology per FAR 15.101-2. Technical proposal is PASS/FAIL gate (acceptable vs unacceptable). Award to LOWEST PRICE among acceptable proposals. Technical superiority beyond "acceptable" does NOT provide competitive advantage. Pricing strategy: Minimum technical compliance (don't over-invest), aggressive price (lowest responsible price), efficiency focus (lean operations, minimal overhead). Avoid: Gold-plating technical solution (waste of proposal effort and inflates price). Technical volume: Demonstrate minimum compliance only - page limits allow, but excellence doesn't win. Focus 80% effort on price optimization, 20% on technical compliance demonstration.
```

**Pricing Strategy Implications**:

- **Race to bottom**: Lowest price wins (period)
- **Minimal technical investment**: Don't waste effort on technical excellence (doesn't count)
- **Pass/fail compliance**: Demonstrate minimum requirements met, nothing more
- **Price optimization**: Reduce overhead, streamline operations, value engineering

## Agency-Specific Evaluation Patterns

> **REMEMBER**: These are TENDENCIES based on historical observation, NOT deterministic rules. Always prioritize what the specific RFP states.

### DoD Evaluation Intelligence

**Decision-making value**: DoD patterns inform win theme development (mission readiness, security, veteran workforce) and past performance content (CPARS ratings critical).

**When RFP indicates DoD agency** (Navy, Air Force, Army, USMC, DLA, etc.):

**Scoring tendency**: Adjectival ratings (Outstanding/Good/Acceptable)  
**Past performance emphasis**: Higher weight (often 30-40%, sometimes equal to technical)  
**CPARS scrutiny**: Anything below "Very Good" = competitive disadvantage

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|Factor 3: Past Performance|evaluation_factor|Past Performance (DoD pattern: typically 30-40% weight, EQUAL TO or HIGHER than civilian 20-30%). CPARS ratings HEAVILY scrutinized - "Exceptional"/"Very Good" = competitive. "Satisfactory" = major weakness. "Marginal"/"Unsatisfactory" = likely non-competitive. Relevance criteria: Similar customer (DoD > Federal > Commercial), similar scope (IT support, cybersecurity, logistics), similar scale ($[X]M contract value, [Y] FTEs), similar complexity (clearances, OCONUS, multi-site). Recency: Within 3-5 years (older = less relevant). Proposal strategy: Lead with highest-rated contracts (Exceptional CPARS), emphasize DoD customer relationships, show scope similarity. If weak CPARS: Address proactively with corrective actions taken, provide context (government shutdown, scope change).
```

**Security clearance criticality**:

- "Will obtain" clearances often unacceptable for critical positions (day-one operational capability required)
- Extract clearance requirements (SECRET, TS, SCI) as MANDATORY requirements
- Already-cleared workforce = competitive discriminator (especially TS/SCI)

**Frequently observed unstated factors**:

- Transition risk (if successor contract) - demonstrate smooth transition capability
- Small business utilization (even on unrestricted) - strong SB plan = positive evaluation
- Mission understanding - show deep knowledge of customer mission, not just technical requirements

**Proposal Content Implications**:

- **Past Performance volume**: 30-40% weight = significant effort (not afterthought)
- **CPARS evidence**: Include CPARS screenshots or narratives in proposal (with permission)
- **Veteran workforce**: Emphasize if applicable (DoD values supporting veterans)
- **Mission language**: Use customer's mission terminology (shows cultural fit)

## Subfactor Intelligence

**Decision-making value**: Subfactor weights determine page allocation within each volume. 35% subfactor gets 35% of volume pages.

### Technical Subfactor Patterns (Common Semantic Meanings)

**Subfactor names vary, recognize semantically**:

**Architecture/Design** (25-40% of technical factor):

- **Semantic meaning**: HOW system will be structured (components, interfaces, data flow)
- **Evaluation focus**: Scalability, security, integration with existing systems, technology stack appropriateness
- **Proposal content**: Architecture diagrams, component descriptions, integration approach, technology justification

**Approach/Methodology** (20-30% of technical factor):

- **Semantic meaning**: HOW work will be executed (processes, procedures, workflows)
- **Evaluation focus**: Alignment with requirements, proven methods, efficiency, innovation
- **Proposal content**: Process flows, methodology description, work breakdown structure, innovation elements

**Risk Mitigation** (25-40% of technical factor, often highest):

- **Semantic meaning**: HOW risks will be identified and managed (proactive risk management)
- **Evaluation focus**: Comprehensive risk identification, realistic mitigation plans, contingency approach
- **Proposal content**: Risk register (10-15 key risks), mitigation strategies, contingency plans, monitoring approach

**Innovation** (10-20% of technical factor):

- **Semantic meaning**: Novel solutions, process improvements, technology insertion
- **Evaluation focus**: Value-add beyond baseline, feasibility, risk-balanced innovation
- **Proposal content**: Innovation examples, proof of concept results, benefit quantification

**Compliance** (15-25% of technical factor):

- **Semantic meaning**: HOW requirements will be met (requirement traceability)
- **Evaluation focus**: Comprehensive requirement understanding, compliance demonstration
- **Proposal content**: Compliance matrix, requirement-by-requirement response, traceability

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:


## Remember

**Evaluation Factor Extraction = Strategic Proposal Planning**:

- Factor weights → Resource allocation decisions (staff, budget, time)
- Subfactor weights → Page allocation decisions (strategic content prioritization)
- Scoring methodology → Competitive strategy (differentiation vs compliance)
- Agency patterns → Win theme development (what government values)

**Enrichment Priority**:

1. **Weight/importance**: Quantify if stated, estimate if not (drives all decisions)
2. **Subfactor structure**: Identify all subfactors + their weights (page allocation)
3. **Scoring methodology**: Adjectival vs numerical (competitive strategy)
4. **Agency pattern**: DoD vs civilian (content emphasis)
5. **Page limits**: Section L linkage (constraint management)

**Quality Check**:

- Does this factor description enable resource allocation decision? (staff size, budget)
- Does it inform page distribution? (subfactor weights)
- Does it suggest competitive strategy? (scoring methodology)
- Does it identify content priorities? (what to emphasize)

If YES to 3+ → excellent enrichment. If NO to most → add more strategic intelligence.
