## Why This Intelligence Matters for Decision Making

**Decision-making value**: Contract vehicle type drives FUNDAMENTAL business decisions:

- **Pricing Strategy**: FFP (firm-fixed-price) = contractor bears cost risk (must price conservatively with contingency). CPFF (cost-plus-fixed-fee) = government bears cost risk (price to actual costs + fee). T&M (time-and-materials) = hybrid risk (government risk on hours, contractor risk on rates). Wrong pricing approach = loss of profit OR non-competitive price.

- **Revenue Predictability**: Firm contract (single award, funded) = predictable revenue ($50M over 5 years guaranteed if perform). IDIQ (multiple awards, unfunded) = uncertain revenue ($100M ceiling but only $2.5K minimum guarantee, actual orders depend on performance/price competition). Affects financial planning and investment decisions.

- **Competition Strategy**: Single award = winner-take-all (compete aggressively, pricing is critical). Multiple award IDIQ = get on contract first, then compete for task orders (different strategy - qualify for IDIQ, then compete on individual tasks). Changes proposal emphasis (IDIQ qualification = demonstrate capability range, task order = demonstrate specific solution + price).

- **Bid/No-Bid Risk Assessment**: IDIQ minimum guarantee ($2.5K) vs ceiling ($100M) = high risk if poor performer (get on contract but win zero task orders). Firm contract = if you win, you execute full scope (lower risk of zero revenue). Risk tolerance affects bid decision.

- **Subcontracting Approach**: IDIQ = often requires teaming agreements BEFORE contract award (teammate pool for unknown future tasks). Firm contract = can identify specific subs based on known scope. Teaming strategy timing differs.

- **Pricing Competitiveness**: GSA Schedule = published ceiling rates (all contractors visible, pricing transparency = must be competitive). Direct contract = sealed pricing (only government sees your price, less transparency). Pricing strategy differs dramatically.

**Without this intelligence**: Price FFP contract like CPFF (don't include contingency = eat losses when costs overrun). Or price IDIQ task order like firm contract (don't account for competition = lose all task orders to lower-priced competitors).

**With this intelligence**: Strategic approach aligned to vehicle ("FFP contract = include 15% contingency for cost risk, CPFF contract = price to costs + 7% fee, IDIQ task order = aggressive pricing to win orders vs other IDIQ holders")

## Contract Vehicle Types

### IDIQ - Indefinite Delivery Indefinite Quantity

**Definition**: Contract for indefinite quantity of supplies/services during fixed period, actual orders placed via task/delivery orders

**Key Characteristics**:

- **Multiple awards common** (5-15 contractors on same IDIQ contract)
- **Minimum guarantee** (typically $2,500 - essentially zero)
- **Maximum ceiling** ($100M, $500M, $1B+ for large IDIQs)
- **Unfunded** (contract award ≠ funding, task orders are funded)
- **Competition within IDIQ** (awarded contractors compete for each task order)
- **5-10 year period of performance** (base + options)

**Decision-making implications**:

**Revenue uncertainty**:

```
IDIQ Contract Award:
- Ceiling: $500M over 10 years
- Minimum guarantee: $2,500
- Actual revenue: UNKNOWN (depends on task order wins)

Scenario 1 (High Performer): Win 30% of task orders = $150M over 10 years ($15M/year)
Scenario 2 (Average Performer): Win 15% of task orders = $75M over 10 years ($7.5M/year)
Scenario 3 (Poor Performer): Win 5% of task orders = $25M over 10 years ($2.5M/year)

Business Decision: IDIQ award is qualification (get on contract), REAL competition is task order level. Must invest in capture for EACH task order (not just initial IDIQ award).
```

**Pricing strategy**:

```
IDIQ Contract Award (Qualification Phase):
- Demonstrate competitive pricing (sample CLINs, labor rates)
- Don't need to be LOWEST (multiple awards = qualify first)
- Show pricing range capability (low-end for junior staff, high-end for senior)

Task Order Competition (Execution Phase):
- Price AGGRESSIVELY (compete against other IDIQ holders)
- Every task order is new competition (cannot rest on contract award)
- Low price often wins (unless best-value allows differentiation)

Strategic Approach: Qualify for IDIQ with competitive rates, then compete hard on individual task orders.
```

**Proposal strategy**:

```
IDIQ Qualification Proposal:
- BROAD capability demonstration (show we can handle wide range of potential tasks)
- Past performance variety (different contract types, scopes, sizes)
- Teaming agreements PRE-AWARD (teammate pool ready for unknown future tasks)
- Capacity demonstration (can scale from $100K tasks to $50M tasks)

Task Order Proposal:
- SPECIFIC solution for this task (narrow focus vs broad IDIQ capability)
- Price competitiveness (other IDIQ holders are competitors)
- Fast response (task order RFPs often 15-30 day response time)

Two different proposal strategies - qualify broadly, execute specifically.
```

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|SeaPort-NX IDIQ Contract|program|Navy IDIQ contract vehicle for engineering, technical, programmatic support services. MULTIPLE AWARD (500+ contractors awarded). Ceiling: $50B over 10 years (2023-2033). Minimum guarantee: $2,500 per contractor (essentially zero revenue guarantee). UNFUNDED: Contract award = qualification only, actual revenue via task order competition among awarded contractors. Task order types: Firm-fixed-price, cost-reimbursable, time-and-materials (varies by task). Competition: Two-phase (1) Qualify for SeaPort-NX contract (broad capability demonstration), (2) Compete for individual task orders (specific solutions + aggressive pricing). Business implication: SeaPort-NX award = door opens (qualification), task order wins = actual revenue (must capture each task individually). Revenue uncertainty: High performers win 20-30% of competed task orders, average performers 10-15%, poor performers <5%. Pricing strategy: Qualify with competitive labor rates ($50-$250/hr range), compete aggressively on task orders (other SeaPort-NX holders are competitors). Proposal strategy: IDIQ qualification = demonstrate broad capability range + past performance variety + teaming pool. Task order proposal = specific solution + competitive price + fast response (15-30 day typical).
```

### BPA - Blanket Purchase Agreement

**Definition**: Simplified acquisition method for recurring services/supplies, establishes pricing/terms for future call orders

**Key Characteristics**:

- **Simplified acquisition** (typically under $250K per call order, though can be higher)
- **Pre-negotiated rates** (labor rates, material pricing established upfront)
- **Call order procedures** (streamlined ordering vs full RFP process)
- **GSA Schedule BPAs common** (BPA established under existing GSA Schedule contract)
- **1-5 year period** (shorter than IDIQ typically)

**Decision-making implications**:

**Pricing advantage**:

```
GSA Schedule BPA:
- Labor rates: Pre-established on GSA Schedule (cannot exceed Schedule ceiling rates)
- BPA may have FURTHER discounts (5-20% off Schedule rates common)
- Predictable pricing: Government knows rates upfront, simplified budgeting

Call Order Pricing:
- Labor hours x BPA rates + materials = total price
- No repricing each order (rates locked for BPA period)
- Government advantage: Fast ordering (no new competition each time)

Strategic Implication: BPA is RELATIONSHIP vehicle (customer commits to use your rates for recurring needs). Revenue more predictable than IDIQ (less competition per order).
```

**Competition pattern**:

```
BPA Establishment Competition:
- Compete to establish BPA (initial selection, often 1-3 BPA holders)
- Demonstrate competitive pricing (rates vs other GSA Schedule holders)
- Show capability for recurring services (past performance with similar needs)

Call Order Competition:
- Minimal or no competition (BPA holder has preferred status)
- If multiple BPA holders: Rotate orders OR compete on specific needs
- Much less competitive than IDIQ task orders (relationship advantage)

Strategic Approach: Win BPA establishment = recurring revenue stream with minimal per-order competition.
```

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|IT Support Services BPA|program|Blanket Purchase Agreement for recurring IT helpdesk and desktop support services. Established under GSA IT Schedule 70 (pre-requisite: must have Schedule 70 contract). BPA ceiling: $25M over 5 years. Pre-negotiated labor rates: Tier 1 Support $45/hr (15% discount off Schedule rate $53/hr), Tier 2 Support $65/hr, Tier 3 Support $95/hr. Call order procedures: Government issues call orders (simplified ordering, no RFP) for specific support needs (number of hours/FTEs), invoices submitted monthly. Competition: Established BPA = preferred vendor status (minimal competition per call order vs IDIQ where every task order is new competition). Revenue predictability: Higher than IDIQ (customer committed to using BPA rates for recurring needs). Pricing locked: Rates fixed for 5-year BPA period (annual escalation 2-3% typical). Strategic value: Recurring revenue stream (customer relationship established, less capture effort per order). Proposal strategy: BPA establishment = demonstrate competitive rates + capability for recurring services + customer service excellence (relationship-focused).
```

### GSA Schedule (Federal Supply Schedule / MAS - Multiple Award Schedule)

**Definition**: Government-wide IDIQ contracts with pre-negotiated pricing for commercial products/services, open to all federal agencies

**Key Characteristics**:

- **Open to all agencies** (any federal agency can order from GSA Schedule)
- **Published ceiling rates** (pricing visible to all agencies, transparency)
- **Commercial item focus** (commercial products/services, not custom development)
- **Fast procurement** (simplified ordering for orders <$250K, competitive for >$250K)
- **5-year base + three 5-year options** (up to 20 years total)

**Decision-making implications**:

**Pricing transparency**:

```
GSA Schedule Pricing Model:
- Ceiling rates published on GSA Advantage (public website)
- All agencies see your rates + competitor rates (full transparency)
- Most Favored Customer (MFC) pricing: Must give government equal-to-or-better pricing than best commercial customer

Pricing Pressure:
- Visible to competitors (can see your rates, adjust theirs accordingly)
- Customer can compare rates across Schedule holders (price shopping)
- Discounting common (offer 10-20% below published ceiling to win orders)

Strategic Implication: Published rates must be COMPETITIVE (cannot hide uncompetitive pricing). Real pricing negotiations happen at order level (discounts off published ceiling).
```

**Market access value**:

```
GSA Schedule Benefits:
- Access to ALL federal agencies (government-wide vehicle)
- Simplified procurement for agencies (don't need to run full competition for small orders)
- Indefinite quantity (no revenue guarantee, but broad market access)

Order Competition:
- <$250K: Agency can direct order (single Schedule holder, minimal competition)
- $250K-$750K: Three quotes required (compete vs other Schedule holders)
- >$750K: Full competition or BPA establishment (more competitive)

Strategic Value: GSA Schedule = MARKET ACCESS vehicle (door opens to 100+ agencies). Revenue depends on marketing + competitive pricing + order capture.
```

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|GSA IT Schedule 70|program|General Services Administration Multiple Award Schedule (MAS) for IT products and services. GOVERNMENT-WIDE vehicle (any federal agency can order). Published pricing: Labor rates and product pricing visible on GSA Advantage public website (full transparency = competitors can see your rates). Order procedures: <$250K = simplified (agency can order directly), $250K-$750K = three quotes required (compete vs other Schedule 70 holders), >$750K = competitive procedures OR BPA establishment. Period of performance: 5-year base + three 5-year options (up to 20 years potential). Revenue model: Unfunded (Schedule award ≠ revenue guarantee), actual revenue via agency orders (must market services + capture individual orders). Pricing constraints: MFC (Most Favored Customer) pricing = must give government equal-or-better pricing than best commercial customer, published ceiling rates must be competitive (visible to all agencies + competitors). Strategic value: MARKET ACCESS (door opens to 100+ federal agencies), BRAND CREDIBILITY (Schedule holder = vetted contractor), FAST PROCUREMENT (agencies prefer Schedule for simplified buying). Revenue generation: Active marketing required (agencies won't find you automatically), competitive pricing critical (transparent rates = easy comparison), order capture essential (Schedule is qualification, orders are revenue). Proposal strategy: Schedule award = demonstrate commercial pricing, past performance, financial stability. Order capture = competitive quotes, relationship building with agency customers, discounts off published ceiling (10-20% common).
```

## Contract Types (Risk Allocation)

### FFP - Firm-Fixed-Price

**Definition**: Contractor performs for fixed price regardless of actual costs, contractor bears cost risk

**Key Characteristics**:

- **Fixed price**: Total contract price established at award, does not change
- **Contractor risk**: If costs exceed price, contractor absorbs loss
- **Government certainty**: Government knows exact cost upfront
- **No cost visibility**: Government doesn't see contractor costs, only pays fixed price

**Decision-making implications**:

**Pricing strategy**:

```
FFP Pricing Formula:
Base Cost (labor + materials + overhead) + Contingency (10-20% for cost risk) + Profit (5-10%) = Fixed Price

Example:
- Estimated costs: $1,000K (labor $700K, materials $200K, overhead $100K)
- Contingency: $150K (15% for cost risk - FFP = contractor eats overruns)
- Profit: $75K (7.5% profit margin)
- Total Fixed Price: $1,225K

Risk Consideration: If actual costs are $1,150K (15% overrun), contractor absorbs $150K loss (contingency burned). If costs are $900K (10% underrun), contractor gains $100K additional profit.

Strategic Implication: FFP = must price conservatively (include contingency for unknowns). Cannot go back to government for more money if costs overrun.
```

**Appropriate uses**:

```
FFP Best For:
- Well-defined requirements (know exactly what work is required)
- Stable scope (minimal change expected)
- Commercial/standard services (known cost basis)
- Contractor experience (done similar work before, can estimate accurately)

FFP Risky For:
- Undefined requirements (unknowns = cost risk)
- Likely scope changes (change orders required for any mod)
- First-of-kind work (cannot estimate accurately)
- Long duration (>2 years = inflation risk, market changes)
```

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|FFP Help Desk Support Contract|concept|Firm-Fixed-Price contract for 24/7 help desk support services. Fixed annual price: $2.4M/year (does not change regardless of actual costs). Contractor cost risk: If actual costs exceed $2.4M (e.g., higher call volume, staff attrition requiring premium pay, infrastructure failures), contractor absorbs losses. Government benefit: Budget certainty (exactly $2.4M/year, predictable). Pricing approach: Estimated base costs $2.0M + 15% contingency $300K (help desk volume uncertainty) + 7.5% profit $150K = $2.4M total. Performance risk: Must deliver help desk services for fixed price (cannot request additional funds if costs increase). Scope protection: Detailed SOW required (any work outside SOW = change order opportunity to adjust price). Strategic consideration: FFP appropriate for help desk (well-defined service, past performance provides accurate cost basis). Monitor costs closely (monthly cost tracking to detect overruns early, take corrective action before losses accumulate).
```

### CPFF - Cost-Plus-Fixed-Fee

**Definition**: Government reimburses contractor's allowable costs plus fixed fee (profit), government bears cost risk

**Key Characteristics**:

- **Cost reimbursement**: Government pays actual costs (labor, materials, overhead)
- **Fixed fee**: Profit amount fixed at award, does not change with costs
- **Government risk**: If costs increase, government pays (not contractor)
- **Cost visibility**: Government audits costs, must be allowable/allocable/reasonable

**Decision-making implications**:

**Pricing strategy**:

```
CPFF Pricing Formula:
Estimated Costs (labor + materials + overhead) + Fixed Fee (7-12% of costs) = CPFF Price

Example:
- Estimated costs: $1,000K (government will reimburse actual costs)
- Fixed fee: $80K (8% fee = contractor profit, FIXED regardless of actual costs)
- Total CPFF Price: $1,080K (estimated)

Risk Allocation:
- Actual costs $1,200K (20% overrun): Government pays $1,200K + $80K fee = $1,280K total
- Actual costs $800K (20% underrun): Government pays $800K + $80K fee = $880K total
- Contractor fee is $80K regardless (fixed profit amount)

Strategic Implication: CPFF = contractor has zero cost risk (government pays actual costs). Profit is fixed dollar amount (not percentage of actuals, so no incentive to overrun costs, but also no penalty).
```

**Appropriate uses**:

```
CPFF Best For:
- Undefined requirements (unknowns = government should bear risk)
- Research & development (cannot predict costs accurately)
- First-of-kind work (no historical cost basis)
- Long duration (5+ years = inflation, market uncertainty)

CPFF Risky For (Government):
- Well-defined work (government paying for contractor inefficiency)
- Commercial services (cost-reimbursement unnecessary for known costs)
- Contractor lack of cost control incentive (guaranteed profit regardless of performance)
```

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|CPFF R&D Contract|concept|Cost-Plus-Fixed-Fee contract for advanced technology research. Estimated costs: $5M (labor $3.5M, materials $1M, overhead $500K). Fixed fee: $400K (8% of estimated costs = contractor profit). Government cost risk: Actual costs may vary significantly (R&D uncertainty), government reimburses ACTUAL allowable costs (not estimated). Contractor profit: $400K fee is FIXED (does not change if costs overrun or underrun). Cost reporting: Monthly cost reports required (labor hours, material purchases, overhead allocation), government visibility into all costs. Allowable cost rules: FAR Part 31 (costs must be allocable, allowable, reasonable), government can disallow unallowable costs (lobbying, entertainment, excessive exec compensation). Strategic consideration: CPFF appropriate for R&D (unknown technical challenges = cannot fix price reasonably, government bears cost risk for innovation). Contractor incentive: Fee is fixed (no benefit from cost overruns, but also no penalty), focus on technical success (not cost minimization). Government control: Cost monitoring required (prevent waste/abuse), contract modifications for scope changes (adjusts estimated costs, may adjust fee).
```

### T&M - Time-and-Materials

**Definition**: Hybrid contract type - government pays for labor hours at fixed hourly rates plus materials at cost, split risk between parties

**Key Characteristics**:

- **Fixed hourly rates**: Labor rates established at award (e.g., $150/hr for senior engineer)
- **Actual hours**: Government pays for actual hours worked (not fixed total)
- **Materials at cost**: Materials billed at actual cost (plus handling fee typically)
- **Ceiling required**: FAR requires not-to-exceed ceiling (protect government from runaway costs)
- **Split risk**: Government bears hours risk (more hours = more cost), contractor bears rate risk (inefficiency not compensated)

**Decision-making implications**:

**Pricing strategy**:

```
T&M Pricing Formula:
Labor Categories: Fixed hourly rates (Senior Engineer $175/hr, Engineer $125/hr, Technician $85/hr)
Hours: Actual hours worked (government pays for what's used)
Materials: Cost + 10% handling fee (standard markup)

Example:
Month 1: 160 hours Senior Engineer ($175 x 160 = $28K) + 320 hours Engineer ($125 x 320 = $40K) + $5K materials = $73K total
Month 2: 120 hours Senior Engineer ($175 x 120 = $21K) + 400 hours Engineer ($125 x 400 = $50K) + $3K materials = $74K total

Risk Split:
- Government risk: If work takes more hours than estimated, government pays (cannot cap hours below ceiling)
- Contractor risk: Rates are fixed (inefficient staff = contractor eats losses, cannot bill for extra time to compensate for poor performance)

Strategic Implication: T&M = government bears utilization risk (more work = more cost), contractor must estimate rates accurately (include inefficiency buffer OR staff efficiently to avoid losses on fixed rates).
```

**Appropriate uses**:

```
T&M Best For:
- Uncertain level of effort (don't know how many hours required)
- Staff augmentation (providing bodies, government directs work)
- Repair/maintenance (unknown failure rates, cannot predict hours)
- Short duration <1 year (longer = should be able to define scope better)

T&M Risky For:
- Well-defined work (should be FFP if scope known)
- Long duration >2 years (FAR discourages long-term T&M)
- Sole-source (T&M = cost-reimbursement characteristics, government should compete)
```

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|T&M IT Support Contract|concept|Time-and-Materials contract for IT support services. Labor rates: Tier 1 Support $65/hr, Tier 2 Support $95/hr, Tier 3 Support $135/hr (rates FIXED for contract period). Estimated hours: 4,000 hours/year (2 FTEs equivalent), but actual hours may vary (government pays for actual usage). Ceiling: $500K/year (not-to-exceed cap per FAR requirement, protects government from runaway costs). Materials: Billed at cost + 10% handling fee (replacement parts, hardware). Risk allocation: Government bears utilization risk (if support requires 5,000 hours vs estimated 4,000 = government pays extra $97.5K within ceiling), contractor bears rate risk (if staff is inefficient and requires 6 hours to fix issue that should take 4 hours = contractor bills 4 hours, absorbs 2 hours loss). Invoice approach: Monthly invoicing (submit timesheets + material receipts, government pays actual hours x rates + materials). Strategic consideration: T&M appropriate for support services (uncertain call volume, cannot predict hours accurately). Government monitoring: Track hours monthly (prevent ceiling breach), ensure work is necessary (T&M = government pays for hours, must verify value). Contractor efficiency: Must staff efficiently (inefficiency = losses, cannot bill extra hours for poor performance).
```

### Incentive Contracts (CPIF, FPIF)

**Definition**: Contract types with performance incentives, share cost savings/overruns between contractor and government

**CPIF - Cost-Plus-Incentive-Fee**:

- Base fee + incentive fee based on cost performance
- Share ratio: 80/20 typical (government gets 80% of savings, contractor gets 20%)
- Minimum/maximum fee (fee range)

**FPIF - Fixed-Price-Incentive-Fee**:

- Target cost, target fee, ceiling price
- Share ratio applies until ceiling hit
- After ceiling, contractor absorbs all overruns (like FFP)

**Decision-making implications**:

**Incentive alignment**:

```
CPIF Example:
- Target cost: $1,000K
- Target fee: $80K (8%)
- Share ratio: 80/20 (government/contractor)
- Minimum fee: $40K (4%)
- Maximum fee: $120K (12%)

Scenario 1 (Underrun): Actual costs $900K
- Savings: $100K
- Contractor share: $100K x 20% = $20K
- Contractor fee: $80K + $20K = $100K (10% fee)
- Government pays: $900K + $100K = $1,000K (vs $1,080K target = $80K savings)

Scenario 2 (Overrun): Actual costs $1,100K
- Overrun: $100K
- Contractor penalty: $100K x 20% = $20K
- Contractor fee: $80K - $20K = $60K (5.5% fee)
- Government pays: $1,100K + $60K = $1,160K (vs $1,080K target = $80K penalty)

Strategic Implication: CPIF = contractor incentivized to control costs (share in savings), but government still bears majority of cost risk (80% of overruns).
```

**Entity Enrichment Pattern**:

```
entity|CPIF Development Contract|concept|Cost-Plus-Incentive-Fee contract for system development. Target cost: $10M, target fee: $800K (8%). Share ratio: 75/25 (government/contractor split on cost variance). Fee range: Minimum $400K (4%), maximum $1.2M (12%). Incentive structure: If actual costs UNDER target = contractor earns higher fee (25% of savings), if OVER target = contractor fee reduced (25% of overrun penalty). Example: Actual costs $9M (10% underrun), savings $1M, contractor bonus $250K (25% of savings), final fee $1.05M (10.5%) = government pays $10.05M total vs $10.8M target (government saves $750K). Ceiling protection: Minimum fee $400K prevents excessive penalty (if costs overrun significantly, fee floors at 4%). Strategic value: Aligns contractor incentives with cost control (profit tied to cost performance), government shares in savings (75% of underruns). Risk: If poor cost estimating at proposal (target too low) = guaranteed fee reduction (actual costs likely exceed low target). Proposal strategy: Realistic target cost essential (sandbagging = government catches in negotiations, aggressive = guaranteed fee penalty).
```

## Remember

**Procurement Vehicle Intelligence = Strategic Alignment**:

- Contract vehicle (IDIQ, BPA, GSA) determines competition pattern (qualify once vs compete per order)
- Contract type (FFP, CPFF, T&M) determines risk allocation (who bears cost overruns?)
- Pricing strategy must align to vehicle/type (FFP = conservative, CPFF = actual costs, T&M = efficient rates)
- Revenue predictability varies (firm contract = guaranteed, IDIQ = uncertain, BPA = recurring)

**Enrichment Priority**:

1. **Vehicle identification**: What procurement vehicle is this? (IDIQ, BPA, GSA, firm contract)
2. **Contract type**: What type? (FFP, CPFF, T&M, incentive)
3. **Risk allocation**: Who bears cost risk? (contractor or government)
4. **Competition pattern**: When do we compete? (qualification + execution, or one-time)
5. **Pricing approach**: How to price? (contingency for FFP, actual costs for CPFF, efficient rates for T&M)

**Quality Check**:

- Is vehicle type identified? (IDIQ vs firm contract = fundamentally different strategies)
- Is contract type clear? (FFP vs CPFF = opposite risk profiles)
- Are pricing implications explained? (FFP = must include contingency)
- Are competition patterns described? (IDIQ = compete twice - qualification + task orders)
- Does intelligence inform decisions? (bid/no-bid, pricing, teaming, revenue forecasting)

If YES to 4+ → excellent vehicle intelligence. If NO to most → add more strategic context.
